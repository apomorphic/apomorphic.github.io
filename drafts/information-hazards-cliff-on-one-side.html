Information hazards: the “cliff on one side” argument

Posted on Wed 24 June 2020 by Will Bradshaw

In EA infohazard discourse, there is a common flavour of pro-secrecy argument that goes something like this:

Existential risks are bad. Really, really bad. So bad that they dominate all other concerns. If being marginally more open with some risky information marginally increases an existential risk, it is likely enormously negative in expectation.

In contrast, the benefits of increased openness mostly revolve around increased scientific and technological progress. More openness means faster diffusion of information, faster and better admixture of different ideas, and generally greater innovation. Decreased openness will cause more researchers to waste their time persuing preventable dead ends, more technologists to miss the crucial insight that they could use to create a revolutionary invention, more team projects to go technically astray. But all of this is a rate change, not a path change: the missing science and technology will be discovered eventually, and humanity will continue along (roughly) the same course. Indeed, in some areas slower technological progress may be actively desirable, if it allows us to better foresee and fend off oncoming dangers.

Finding the optimal level of openness is very difficult, but the costs of being too open are far smaller than the costs of being too closed. We are walking along a ridge, and there is a yawning chasm on one side and a rocky but relatively gentle slope on the other. Insofar as we are not omniscient, we should err strongly on the side of talking less.
