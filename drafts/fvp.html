Final Version Perfected: An Underused Execution Algorithm

Posted on Mon 12 October 2020 by Will Bradshaw

  • Intro: Planning vs Execution Systems
  • The FVP Algorithm
  • How to do FVP in practice (online vs paper)
  • Why I think FVP is so effective

The world is awash with productivity systems.

Sometimes it seems like everyone and their mother has come up with some byzantine new method for organising your time: how to organise your To-Do List, organise your calendar, organise your projects.

Probably the most well-known such system is Getting Things Done (GTD), which is essentially an elaboration and formalisation of the To-Do List. I’ve used GTD extensively in the past, and many of my systems are still based around its advice.

GTD, though, is a planning system: its job is to make sure all the tasks you might want to do are captured and presented to you in a helpful manner. Like many productivity systems, it’s quite bad at actually helping you do the the right task in a given moment.

Execution is the Last Mile Problem of productivity infrastructure. You can put as much effort as you like into organising your goals, organising your To-Do lists, organising your calendar, but sooner or later you will be presented with more than one thing you could reasonably be doing with your time. When that happens, you will need some sort of method for choosing what that thing will be, and actually getting started.

That method needs to be quick to execute, easy to remember, and effective at actually having you do the best task for the time. It needs to be robust to your psychological weaknesses, minimising procrastination, indecision, and ugh fields. It needs to be efficient, performing only the work needed to identify the most valuable task1.

Enter Final Version Perfected.

2. The FVP Algorithm

The algorithm for executing tasks under FVP2 is pretty simple. You can find a description of it by the designer here3, but here’s my version:

  1. Put all the tasks you have to choose from4 into one big unsorted list.
  2. Mark the first item on the list. Don’t do it yet.
  3. For each subsequent item on the list, ask yourself, “Do I want5 to do this task more than the last task I marked?” If yes, mark it. If no, don’t mark it. Move on to the next item.
  4. When you reach the end of the list, trace back up the list to select the lowest marked task. Do it, then cross it off the list.
  5. Beginning with the task after the task you just crossed off, repeat step 3, comparing each task to the last uncrossed task you marked (i.e. the one prior to the one you just crossed out).
  6. Go to step 4. Repeat until you run out of time or list items.

3. FVP: Why and Why Not

The effectiveness of FVP depends on three assumptions:

  • Firstly, the algorithm assumes that your preferences are transitive: that, if you want to do B more than A, and C more than B, you want to do C more than A. In reality, your preferences probably aren’t quite transitive, but hopefully they’re close enough that pretending they are is reasonable. As long as transitivity holds, FVP is guaranteed to identify the single best task for you to do; if not, well, you have bigger problems.

  • Secondly, it assumes that your preferences are stable over the timeframe you’re using it. If your preferences shift substantially over that period, you’ll need to throw out your previous FVP and start again7. This places some constraints on the timescale you can organise using a single FVP iteration: I seldom stick with the same iteration for longer than a day8.

  • Thirdly, and more weakly, the value of FVP assumes that you are unsure about which task you should do next. I find FVP most useful during long, fairly unstructured periods when I have a lot of different tasks competing for my time. If my time is pre-allocated to a single, extended, fairly unitary task (say, studying a topic, or writing a chapter) FVP is much less useful – I already have enough context in my head to easily identify what I should be doing next.

When these three conditions are met, FVP is a very effective method for guiding action. It is both efficient and exhaustive: guaranteed to identify the top-priority task while avoiding most of the work involved in producing a complete ranking. It is a simple algorithm, easy to remember and easy to carry out – though I didn’t yet find a digital tool that is as good for FVP as pen-and-paper. After doing it for a while, I find it scarcely requires conscious thought – but still reliably identifies the most valuable task for me to work on.

The biggest benefit I get from FVP, though, is how much easier it makes it to do important things I’d rather avoid. There is something about a bald, pairwise comparison between two tasks that is highly effective at overcoming my aversion to difficult things. If an important but unpleasant task is nestled within a long to-do list of minor but rewarding busywork, it is easy for my eye to skip over the difficult task, defer it till tomorrow, and work on something more pleasant instead. It’s much harder to do that when comparing the important task to each minor task in isolation.

FVP is also good at minimising time lost due to indecision. When presented with a menu of tasks to choose from, it can be quite hard to select a single task to work on first. When that choice process is reduced to a series of simple pairwise comparisons, the choosing process as a whole becomes much easier. And, once I’ve finished with FVP and selected a single winning task, there’s an impetus towards starting that makes me much less prone to procrastinating.
